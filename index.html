Empowering Sustainability and Circularity

The Extended Producer Responsibility Association (EPRA) is driving India’s transition to a circular economy by promoting responsible waste management and sustainability. By bringing together producers, recyclers, policymakers, and industries, we are setting global benchmarks in Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR).

Global EPR Practices

European Union: Comprehensive Regulations and Circular Economy Focus

The European Union has one of the most advanced EPR frameworks, incorporating packaging, electronics, batteries, and textiles into its Circular Economy Action Plan. The Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Directive mandates that manufacturers finance the collection and recycling of electronic products. The Single-Use Plastics Directive holds producers accountable for plastic waste, requiring them to contribute to collection and recycling systems. The Battery Regulation enforces take-back programs for all battery types, ensuring material recovery. The EU also promotes eco-modulation fees, meaning producers pay less if their products are easily recyclable, creating an incentive for sustainable design.

United States: State-Led EPR Initiatives

The United States does not have a federal EPR mandate, but several states have implemented their own regulations. California, Oregon, and Maine have EPR laws requiring producers to fund recycling programs for packaging. Over 25 states have e-waste regulations mandating manufacturers to collect and recycle electronics. Some states have additional take-back programs for hazardous materials like paints and batteries, funded by industry contributions. While EPR is expanding, the fragmented approach across states makes nationwide implementation challenging.

Canada: Producer Responsibility Organization (PRO) Model

Canada operates a Producer Responsibility Organization model, where producers finance waste management programs through industry-led initiatives. Provinces like British Columbia and Ontario require full producer responsibility for packaging, ensuring businesses contribute to collection and recycling efforts. Canada is also expanding EPR programs to cover textiles and hazardous household waste, creating a more comprehensive waste management system.

Japan: High Recycling Rates and Strict EPR Policies

Japan has one of the most efficient recycling systems globally, supported by strict EPR laws. The Home Appliance Recycling Law mandates that consumers pay a recycling fee when purchasing electronics to ensure proper disposal. The Automobile Recycling Law requires manufacturers to recover and reuse parts such as airbags and batteries. Japan also leads in food waste recycling, converting organic waste into bioenergy and animal feed, significantly reducing landfill waste.

South Korea: Mandatory Recycling Targets and Deposit Refund System

South Korea enforces strict EPR regulations, requiring manufacturers to meet mandatory recycling quotas for packaging waste. Failure to meet these targets results in financial penalties. The country operates a deposit-refund system for beverage containers, encouraging consumers to return bottles for recycling. South Korea’s food waste-to-energy program ensures organic waste is repurposed into biofuel and compost, making it a leader in sustainable waste management.

China: Strengthening EPR for Industrial and Plastic Waste

China is rapidly strengthening its EPR regulations to tackle rising waste levels. Electronics manufacturers are required to implement take-back programs for e-waste. The Plastic Pollution Control Action Plan bans certain single-use plastics and mandates that producers contribute to waste collection efforts. Industrial waste generators must track and manage hazardous waste more effectively to comply with national sustainability goals.

ISCC Certification: Ensuring Sustainable and Traceable Supply Chains

The International Sustainability and Carbon Certification (ISCC) system plays a significant role in global EPR initiatives by certifying sustainable supply chains for bio-based and recycled materials. ISCC certification ensures that products meet environmental and social sustainability criteria, helping businesses align with circular economy goals. Many EPR programs, especially in the European Union, require ISCC certification for compliance with sustainability regulations, particularly in packaging, plastics, and biofuel sectors.

Emerging Trends in Global EPR Practices

Countries are increasingly integrating digital tracking systems for waste monitoring, allowing better enforcement and transparency. Incentives for sustainable product design, such as eco-modulation fees and recyclability scoring, are gaining traction. Carbon credit mechanisms linked to EPR programs are being explored to further incentivize waste reduction and recycling. Strengthening policy enforcement, investing in advanced recycling infrastructure, and expanding producer responsibility beyond traditional waste streams will be key to making EPR more effective worldwide.

About us

Driving India’s Sustainability Goals Through EPR

The EPR Association (EPRA) was launched at the India Energy Week 2025 in New Delhi, to bring together producers, importers, recyclers, and policymakers to create a structured and effective EPR ecosystem in India. Our alliance ensures industries meet regulatory obligations, promotes circular economy practices, and enables compliance with waste management laws for plastics, e-waste, batteries, and other sectors.

With India generating over 62 million tonnes of waste annually, of which 50% ends up in landfills, there is an urgent need for sustainable solutions. Through policy advocacy, industry collaboration, and technological innovation, EPRA is leading the way in addressing waste management challenges and reducing carbon footprints.
